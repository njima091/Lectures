Class 4: Concentration and Fluctuations of Passage Times

In this class, we explore the concentration and fluctuations of passage times in First Passage Percolation (FPP). Beyond the average behavior described by the time constant and the asymptotic shape, understanding these fluctuations is crucial for a complete picture of FPP.

Understanding Fluctuations in First Passage Percolation

While the law of large numbers and the Shape Theorem provide insights into the asymptotic behavior of FPP, they don't tell the complete story. In reality, passage times fluctuate around their expected values, and understanding the nature of these fluctuations reveals deeper aspects of the model.

Variance of Passage Times

The variance of the passage time $T(0, nv)$ quantifies how much the actual passage times typically deviate from their expected value. Here, $T(0, nv)$ represents the passage time from the origin to a point $nv$ at distance $n$ in direction $v$.

The study of these fluctuations helps us understand how the inherent randomness at the microscopic level (individual edge weights) manifests in the macroscopic behavior of the system.

Kesten's Variance Bound

One of the most significant contributions to understanding the fluctuations of passage times comes from Harry Kesten, who established an important upper bound on the variance.

Kesten's Upper Bound

Kesten proved that the variance of the passage time $T(0, nv)$ is bounded above by a constant multiplied by the distance:

$$\text{Var}(T(0, nv)) \leq C \cdot n$$

where $C$ is a constant that depends on the distribution of the edge weights.

This linear growth of variance with distance indicates that the fluctuations of passage time around its mean value do not grow faster than linearly with the distance traveled. This result suggests that the passage time, while random, does not exhibit wildly erratic behavior and tends to stay relatively close to its expected value.

Alexander's Method

Alexander's method is a powerful technique used in the study of disordered systems, including first passage percolation.

The Method

This approach involves introducing a small perturbation to the random environment, such as slightly altering the weight of a single edge in the lattice, and then studying how this localized change affects global quantities like the first passage time between distant points.

By carefully relating the sensitivity of passage times to applied perturbations, Alexander's method provides insights into the nature and magnitude of fluctuations. It allows researchers to establish connections between local stochasticity and global fluctuations in the system.

This approach highlights the importance of understanding the stability of geodesics and passage times under small perturbations in the underlying random medium.

Example: The Benjamini-Kalai-Schramm (BKS) Inequality

The BKS inequality is a general and widely applicable tool within percolation theory that provides an upper bound on the probability of the union of a collection of "local" events.

The strength of this inequality lies in its ability to bound the probability in terms of the probabilities of individual events occurring independently.

Application to FPP:

In the context of first passage percolation, the BKS inequality can demonstrate that the passage time $T(0, nv)$ is concentrated around its mean value. This means that the probability of observing passage times that deviate significantly from the expected value decays rapidly as the magnitude of the deviation increases.

The BKS inequality provides a way to transfer information about the probabilities of local events (related to individual edge weights or small regions of the lattice) to global concentration results concerning macroscopic quantities like the total passage time over large distances.

Newman-Piza Lower Bound

While Kesten's result provides an upper bound on the variance, Newman and Piza derived a complementary lower bound using the Aizenman-Wahr inequality from standard percolation theory.

Lower Bound on Variance

Under certain conditions on the distribution of edge weights, the Newman-Piza result suggests that the variance of the passage time typically grows at least linearly with the distance $n$.

This indicates that the fluctuations in passage times are not arbitrarily small and have a growth rate that is at least on the order of $n$.

Combining the upper bound from Kesten and the lower bound from Newman and Piza provides a more precise characterization of the typical size of deviations of the passage time from its expected value as a function of the distance traveled.

$$c \cdot n \leq \text{Var}(T(0, nv)) \leq C \cdot n$$

This suggests that the variance of passage times in FPP typically scales linearly with distance.

Comparison: Concentration Results in FPP

Result/Method Key Insight Implication for FPP
Kesten's Variance Bound Var(T(0, nv)) ≤ C·n Fluctuations grow at most linearly with distance
Alexander's Method Small perturbations affect global quantities Links local changes to global fluctuations
BKS Inequality Bounds on probabilities of unions of events Demonstrates concentration around mean value
Newman-Piza Lower Bound Var(T(0, nv)) ≥ c·n Fluctuations grow at least linearly with distance
Combined Results c·n ≤ Var(T(0, nv)) ≤ C·n Variance scales linearly with distance

Key Insights about Concentration in FPP

  • The variance of passage times grows linearly with distance, neither faster nor slower.
  • This linear scaling is fundamentally different from the behavior observed in exactly solvable models of last passage percolation, where variances typically scale as n2/3.
  • Understanding these fluctuations is crucial for applications of FPP in modeling real-world phenomena.
  • The concentration results provide bounds on how much the actual passage times can deviate from their expected values.
  • These fluctuations have implications for the roughness of the "growing surface" in the FPP growth model and connect to the KPZ universality class in statistical physics.
