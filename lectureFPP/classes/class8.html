Class 8: Second Derivative Test for Functions of Two Variables

In this class, we will develop the second derivative test for functions of two variables, which provides a systematic way to classify critical points as local maxima, local minima, or saddle points.

The Hessian Matrix for Functions of Two Variables

For a function $f(x,y)$ of two variables, the Hessian matrix at a point $(x_0, y_0)$ is a $2 \times 2$ matrix of all second-order partial derivatives:

$$H(x,y) = \begin{bmatrix} f_{xx}(x,y) & f_{xy}(x,y) \\ f_{yx}(x,y) & f_{yy}(x,y) \end{bmatrix}$$

Where $f_{xx}$ denotes $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}$, $f_{xy}$ denotes $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}$, and so on.

Note that if $f$ has continuous second partial derivatives, then $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ (this is known as Clairaut's theorem or Schwarz's theorem). This means the Hessian matrix is symmetric.

Second Derivative Test for Functions of Two Variables

Let $f(x,y)$ be a function with continuous second partial derivatives, and let $(x_0, y_0)$ be a critical point of $f$ (i.e., $\nabla f(x_0, y_0) = \mathbf{0}$). Define:

$$D(x_0, y_0) = f_{xx}(x_0, y_0) \cdot f_{yy}(x_0, y_0) - [f_{xy}(x_0, y_0)]^2$$

This is the determinant of the Hessian matrix at $(x_0, y_0)$. The second derivative test states:

  1. If $D(x_0, y_0) > 0$ and $f_{xx}(x_0, y_0) > 0$, then $(x_0, y_0)$ is a local minimum.
  2. If $D(x_0, y_0) > 0$ and $f_{xx}(x_0, y_0) < 0$, then $(x_0, y_0)$ is a local maximum.
  3. If $D(x_0, y_0) < 0$, then $(x_0, y_0)$ is a saddle point.
  4. If $D(x_0, y_0) = 0$, the test is inconclusive and further analysis is needed.

The reasoning behind this test comes from analyzing the behavior of the quadratic approximation of the function near the critical point, which is captured by the Hessian matrix.

Example 8.1

Question: Classify the critical point of $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - 2x - 4y + 5$.

Solution:

In Example 7.1, we found that $(1, 2)$ is the only critical point of this function. Now, let's compute the second derivatives:

$$f_{xx}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x - 2) = 2$$ $$f_{yy}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2y - 4) = 2$$ $$f_{xy}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2y - 4) = 0$$

Computing the determinant at the critical point $(1, 2)$:

$$D(1, 2) = f_{xx}(1, 2) \cdot f_{yy}(1, 2) - [f_{xy}(1, 2)]^2 = 2 \cdot 2 - 0^2 = 4 > 0$$

Since $D(1, 2) > 0$ and $f_{xx}(1, 2) = 2 > 0$, the critical point $(1, 2)$ is a local minimum according to the second derivative test.

We can also verify this by rewriting the function in a more revealing form:

$$f(x,y) = x^2 - 2x + y^2 - 4y + 5 = (x - 1)^2 + (y - 2)^2 + 5 - 1 - 4 = (x - 1)^2 + (y - 2)^2 + 0$$

This form clearly shows that $f(x,y) \geq 0$ for all $(x,y)$, with equality only at $(1, 2)$, confirming it's a global minimum.

Example 8.2

Question: Classify the critical point of $f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2 + 2x$.

Solution:

In Example 7.2, we found that $(-1, 0)$ is the only critical point of this function. Let's compute the second derivatives:

$$f_{xx}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(2x + 2) = 2$$ $$f_{yy}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-2y) = -2$$ $$f_{xy}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(-2y) = 0$$

Computing the determinant at the critical point $(-1, 0)$:

$$D(-1, 0) = f_{xx}(-1, 0) \cdot f_{yy}(-1, 0) - [f_{xy}(-1, 0)]^2 = 2 \cdot (-2) - 0^2 = -4 < 0$$

Since $D(-1, 0) < 0$, the critical point $(-1, 0)$ is a saddle point according to the second derivative test, confirming our conclusion from Example 7.2.

Applications in Engineering

The second derivative test is crucial in various engineering applications:

  • Structural Analysis: Finding stable and unstable equilibrium configurations of 2D mechanical systems.
  • Optimization: Identifying optimal designs or operating conditions for 2D engineered systems.
  • Signal Processing: Detecting local features in 2D signals and images.
  • Machine Learning: Finding optimal parameters in 2D parameter spaces and understanding the convergence properties of optimization methods.

Key Insights about the Second Derivative Test for Functions of Two Variables

  • The Hessian matrix encapsulates all the second-order behavior of a function near a critical point.
  • For functions of two variables, the determinant of the Hessian (along with the sign of $f_{xx}$) determines the type of critical point.
  • The test can be inconclusive in certain cases, requiring further analysis.
  • Understanding the nature of critical points is essential for optimization problems in engineering, where finding minima, maxima, or saddle points often has practical significance.
  • The second derivative test provides a systematic approach to classify critical points without having to examine the behavior of the function in all directions.
