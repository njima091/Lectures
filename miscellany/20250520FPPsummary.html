Scaling Relations in Random Path Models

Setup

Let $$ \Gamma_n(x) = \left\{ (x_i)_{i=1}^n : x_i \in \mathbb{Z}^d,\, |x_i - x_{i-1}|_1 = 0,\ x_0 = 0,\ x_n = x \right\}. $$ To each vertex $(n, x)$, where $n \in \mathbb{N}$ and $x \in \mathbb{Z}^d$, assign an independent continuous random variable $\omega_{n,x}$. Define $$ L_n(x) = \sup_{\gamma \in \Gamma_n(x)} \sum_{x' \in \gamma} \tau_{x'}. $$ Let $G_n(x)$ denote the unique geodesic $\gamma \in \Gamma_n(x)$ attaining $L_n(x)$.

Known limit: $$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} L_n(x) = \phi(x) $$ for some function $\phi(x)$.

Fluctuation and Transversal Exponent

  • Rate of convergence: $R_n(x) = |L_n(x) - n\,\phi(x)|$
  • Transversal deviation: $D_n(x) = \sup_{x' \in G_n(x)} |x'|$

Our aim is to show that for $d=2$, $$ R_n(x) \approx n^{1/3},\qquad D_n(x) \approx n^{2/3}. $$

Assumptions

  1. For small $t$, $\phi(e_1 + t e_2) \leq \phi(e_1) - c t^2$ for some $c > 0$ independent of $t$.
  2. The orders of $R_n(x)$ and $D_n(x)$ are approximately independent of the direction $x$. For brevity, write $R_n$ and $D_n$ for these quantities.

Scaling Relation

We first consider that $$ R_n \approx \frac{(D_n)^2}{n}. $$ To see this, note the following computation:

\begin{align*} \Omega(R_n) &= L(n e_1 + D_n e_2) - L(n e_1) \tag{*} \\ &= L(n e_1 + D_n e_2) - \phi(n e_1) - (L(n e_1) - \phi(n e_1)) \\ &= L(n e_1 + D_n e_2) - \phi(n e_1 + D_n e_2) - (L(n e_1) - \phi(n e_1)) + \Omega(D_n^2/n) \\ &= \Omega(R_n) + \Omega(D_n^2/n). \end{align*}

Hence, it is reasonable to balance these terms: $$ \Omega(R_n) = \Omega\left( \frac{D_n^2}{n} \right). \tag{SR} $$

1/3 Exponent

Define the increments: $$ X^n_i = L(n e_1 + i e_2) - L(n e_1 + (i-1) e_2). $$ Suppose (heuristically) that $X^n_i$ are i.i.d., centered random variables. By the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), we have:

L(n e_1 + D_n e_2) - L(n e_1) = \sum_{i=1}^{D_n} X^n_i = \Omega(\sqrt{D_n})

By the calculations above and the scaling relation (SR), $$ \Omega(R_n) = \Omega\left( \frac{D_n^2}{n} \right) = \Omega(\sqrt{D_n}) $$ which leads to $$ D_n = \Omega(n^{2/3}),\quad R_n = \Omega(n^{1/3}). $$
