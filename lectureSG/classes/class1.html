Mathematical Formulation

Let's introduce the formal mathematical framework for First Passage Percolation:

Consider a graph $G = (V, E)$ with vertex set $V$ and edge set $E$. For each edge $e \in E$, we assign a non-negative random weight $\tau_e$, where the weights are drawn independently from a common distribution $F$.

For a path $P = (v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_n)$ in the graph, the passage time $T(P)$ is defined as:

$T(P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \tau_{(v_{i-1},v_i)}$

The first passage time $T(x,y)$ between vertices $x$ and $y$ is then:

$T(x,y) = \inf\{T(P) : P \text{ is a path from } x \text{ to } y\}$

Background and History of FPP

This lecture explores the origins, development, and historical context of First Passage Percolation (FPP), tracing its evolution from its introduction to its current status as a fundamental area in probability theory.

Origins and Motivation

The formal introduction of first passage percolation as a mathematical model occurred in 1965, attributed to the seminal work of John Hammersley and Dominic Welsh. Their initial motivation stemmed from the practical problem of mathematically describing the flow of fluids through inhomogeneous or porous materials. In this context, the random weights assigned to the edges of the lattice can be interpreted as representing the varying degrees of permeability within the medium.

The Original Problem

Hammersley and Welsh sought to model:

  • How quickly fluid would percolate through a random medium
  • How the microscopic structure of a medium affects macroscopic flow properties
  • The relationship between random local travel times and global flow patterns

Early Research Focus

Early research efforts in the field primarily concentrated on the model defined on the two-dimensional square lattice, Z2, largely due to its relative mathematical simplicity and tractability. However, the fundamental framework of FPP has since been extended and applied to a wider variety of more general graph structures.

FPP is now recognized as a cornerstone within probability theory, holding a significant place as one of its most classical and fundamental areas, with close ties to the broader field of percolation theory. In fact, classical Bernoulli percolation, where edges are either open or closed with certain probabilities, can be considered as a specific instance of FPP where the edge weights take only the values 0 or 1.

Connection to Classical Percolation

Bernoulli percolation can be viewed as a special case of FPP:

  • In Bernoulli percolation, each edge is "open" with probability p, and "closed" with probability 1-p
  • This corresponds to an FPP model where edge weights are either 0 (open) or ∞ (closed)
  • Questions about connectivity in Bernoulli percolation can be reframed as questions about finite passage times in FPP

Key Developments and Driving Questions

A pivotal question that was raised in the original 1969 paper by Hammersley and Welsh concerned the long-term behavior of the set of points reachable from the origin within a given time t, formally denoted as B(t) = {y ∈ Z2 : T(0,y) ≤ t}, as t approaches infinity.

$$B(t) = \{y \in \mathbb{Z}^2 : T(0,y) \leq t\}$$

This question has served as a major driving force behind much of the subsequent research conducted in the field of FPP. This initial inquiry eventually led to the groundbreaking Cox-Durrett limit shape theorem in 1981, a result that significantly advanced the understanding of the large-scale geometric properties of FPP.

The shape theorem, informally stated, says that as t grows large, the set B(t), when scaled down by t, converges to a deterministic, convex, compact shape. This is analogous to the law of large numbers for random variables, but in a geometric context.

Mathematical Impact

Interestingly, the investigation of FPP has not only yielded results specific to the model itself but has also contributed to the development of important mathematical tools. Most notably, the Subadditive Ergodic Theorem, a fundamental result in ergodic theory, emerged partially from the study of FPP. This highlights the broader impact of FPP beyond its specific applications and demonstrates its role in the advancement of mathematical theory.

The connection between a concrete physical problem and the development of an abstract mathematical model illustrates how practical considerations can inspire theoretical advancements. The fact that a question posed in the 1960s led to a major theorem in the 1980s, and that many questions remain unanswered, underscores the enduring challenges and the ongoing nature of research in FPP.

Subadditive Ergodic Theorem

The Subadditive Ergodic Theorem, which generalizes the usual ergodic theorem, provides a powerful tool for analyzing the asymptotic behavior of subadditive processes. In the context of FPP, it is used to establish the existence of the time constant, a fundamental quantity that characterizes the large-scale behavior of the model.
