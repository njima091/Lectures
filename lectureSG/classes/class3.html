Class 3: The Shape Theorem and Limit Shapes

In this class, we explore the Shape Theorem, a fundamental result in First Passage Percolation that describes the asymptotic behavior of the set of reachable points. We'll examine Kesten's large deviation estimates that paved the way for this theorem and discuss conjectures regarding properties of the limit shape.

Kesten's Large Deviation Estimates

Harry Kesten made significant contributions to the field of first passage percolation. Among his many important results are large deviation estimates for the passage time, which provide quantitative bounds on the probability of significant deviations from expected values.

Large Deviation Estimates

Kesten derived estimates that bound the probability that the first passage time $T(0, nv)$ deviates significantly from its expected value, which is approximately $n\mu(v)$ for large values of $n$:

$$P(|T(0, nv) - n\mu(v)| > \lambda n) \leq C_1e^{-C_2\lambda^2 n}$$

for suitable constants $C_1, C_2 > 0$ and for sufficiently small $\lambda > 0$.

These estimates demonstrate that the probability of large deviations decays exponentially with the distance $n$. The specific mathematical form depends on the probability distribution governing the random edge weights.

Kesten's large deviation results offer a more refined understanding of the fluctuations of passage times beyond the information provided by the law of large numbers. They quantify the likelihood of observing rare events where the actual time taken to travel a large distance is substantially longer or shorter than the average expected time.

The Shape Theorem: Statement and Implications

The Shape Theorem (Cox-Durrett, 1981)

Let $B(t) = \{x \in \mathbb{Z}^d : T(0, x) \leq t\}$ be the set of all points reachable from the origin within time $t$. Then, for any arbitrarily small positive value $\varepsilon$, there exists a random time $T_\varepsilon$ such that for all times $t > T_\varepsilon$, with probability one:

$$(1-\varepsilon)R \subset \frac{B(t)}{t} \subset (1+\varepsilon)R$$

Here, $R$ is a non-empty, compact, and convex set in $\mathbb{R}^d$ that is symmetric about the origin, known as the limit shape.

Interpretation and Significance

The Shape Theorem reveals a remarkable regularity in the large-scale behavior of FPP:

  • Despite the inherent randomness at the local level, the overall shape of the reachable region, when appropriately rescaled, converges to a fixed, deterministic shape as time becomes very large.
  • The limit shape $R$ can be characterized in terms of the time constant: $R = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^d : \mu(x) \leq 1\}$.
  • This is analogous to the Law of Large Numbers but in a geometric context.
  • The properties of the limit shape (non-empty, compact, convex, symmetric) provide crucial information about the nature of growth and propagation within the random medium.

Key Elements of the Shape Theorem Proof

While a complete proof is beyond the scope of this course, we outline the main steps:

  1. Subadditivity of the passage time function allows application of the Subadditive Ergodic Theorem, establishing the existence of the time constant $\mu(v)$.
  2. Large deviation estimates bound the probability that the passage time deviates significantly from its mean.
  3. The ergodic nature of the i.i.d. edge weights ensures that "typical" behavior eventually dominates.
  4. Geometric arguments establish that the resulting limit shape has the properties of being non-empty, compact, convex, and symmetric.
  5. Technical arguments then show that with probability 1, the scaled set $B(t)/t$ eventually stays within $(1±\varepsilon)R$ for all sufficiently large $t$.
Example 3.1: Limit Shape in Two-dimensional First Passage Percolation

Consider FPP on $\mathbb{Z}^2$ where edge weights follow an exponential distribution with parameter 1.

Despite the inherent randomness in this model, the Shape Theorem guarantees that the set of points reachable within time $t$, when scaled by $1/t$, converges to a deterministic shape.

While we can't determine the exact form of this limit shape analytically, simulations and theory suggest it is a convex, symmetric set. Under this specific distribution, the limit shape is not a perfect circle due to the underlying lattice structure's anisotropy.

This example illustrates how the macroscopic behavior (the limit shape) emerges from microscopic randomness (individual edge weights), demonstrating the power of the Shape Theorem in revealing order within a random system.

Conjectures on the Limit Shape

While the Shape Theorem definitively establishes the existence of a limit shape, many of its detailed geometric properties remain unknown and are the subject of ongoing research.

Strict Convexity Conjecture

It is widely believed that under mild conditions on the probability distribution of the edge weights (for instance, if the distribution is not concentrated on a single value), the limit shape $R$ should be strictly convex.

Strict convexity implies that the boundary of the set $R$ does not contain any straight line segments. If this conjecture holds true, it would suggest that for any given direction of travel, there is a unique "optimal" asymptotic direction to move in to minimize the passage time per unit distance.

Uniform Curvature Conjecture

Another significant conjecture proposes that under similar mild conditions on the edge weight distribution, the boundary of the limit shape $R$ should possess uniform positive curvature at all points where it is differentiable.

This would imply a certain degree of smoothness in the shape of the boundary, without sharp corners or flat segments.

These two conjectures are known to be quite challenging to prove in their full generality and remain open problems in most cases. However, there has been progress made in specific scenarios.

Recent Progress

In 2016, Auffinger and Damron achieved a significant result by proving the existence of at least one point on the boundary of the limit shape where it is differentiable, specifically in the case where the edge weight distribution allows for the possibility of "flat edges."

This partial result provides some insight into the geometric nature of the limit shape, though the full conjectures remain unresolved.

The open nature of the strict convexity and uniform curvature conjectures highlights the difficulties in fully characterizing the precise geometric nature of the limit shape in FPP. These conjectures continue to motivate and guide ongoing research efforts in the field, as their resolution would provide a much deeper and more complete understanding of the geometric properties of the asymptotic shape that emerges in first passage percolation.

Summary: The Shape Theorem's Place in FPP

The Shape Theorem represents one of the most fundamental results in the theory of first passage percolation. It establishes that despite the randomness at the microscopic level, there emerges a deterministic, predictable behavior at the macroscopic scale.

This result is particularly striking because it demonstrates how order can emerge from chaos - the random assignment of edge weights ultimately leads to a regular, deterministic shape when viewed at a large enough scale. This theme of "regularity from randomness" is common in probability theory and statistical physics.

From a historical perspective, the Shape Theorem represents the successful resolution of the question posed by Hammersley and Welsh in their 1969 paper about the long-term behavior of the set B(t). The result, proven by Cox and Durrett in 1981, has since become a cornerstone of FPP theory.

The study of the geometric properties of the limit shape continues to be an active area of research, with the strict convexity and uniform curvature conjectures representing some of the most important open problems in the field.
