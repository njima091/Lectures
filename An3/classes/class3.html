Class 3: Tangent Planes

In this class, we introduce tangent planes to surfaces $z=f(x,y)$, deriving their equation using partial derivatives. The tangent plane provides the best linear approximation to the surface at a point, similar to how tangent lines approximate curves.

Tangent Lines: A Review

Tangent Line

Given a function $f(x)$ and $a\in \mathbb{R}$, we define the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(a,f(a))$ as the line through the point $(a,f(a))$ with slope $f'(a)$:

$$y = f'(a)(x-a)+f(a)$$

Interpretation from derivative: around $x=a$, $f(x)$ is approximately $f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)$. More precisely, we have the following theorem:

$$f(x)-(f(a)+f'(a)(x-a)) = o(|x-a|)$$

where $o(|x-a|)$ is a function that goes to 0 faster than $|x-a|$ as $x$ approaches $a$, i.e., $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{o(|x-a|)}{|x-a|} = 0$.

Definition of Tangent Planes

Tangent Plane

Consider the surface $z=f(x,y)$. Given a point $(a,b)$, we define the tangent plane to the surface at the point $(a,b,f(a,b))$ as the plane through the point $(a,b,f(a,b))$ with slope $f_x(a,b)$ in the x-direction and $f_y(a,b)$ in the y-direction. The equation of this plane is given by:

$$z=f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$
Example 3.1

Find the tangent plane to the paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$ at the point $(1,2,5)$.

Solution:

Here $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$. First, we compute the partial derivatives:

\begin{align*} f_x(x,y) &= 2x\\ f_y(x,y) &= 2y \end{align*}

At the point $(a,b)=(1,2)$, we have:

\begin{align*} f_x(1,2) &= 2(1) = 2\\ f_y(1,2) &= 2(2) = 4 \end{align*}

The surface passes through $(1,2,f(1,2)) = (1,2,1^2+2^2) = (1,2,5)$ as given.

Thus, the tangent plane equation is:

\begin{align*} z - 5 &= 2(x-1) + 4(y-2)\\ z - 5 &= 2x - 2 + 4y - 8\\ z &= 2x + 4y - 5 \end{align*}

This plane touches the paraboloid $z=x^2+y^2$ at the point $(1,2,5)$ and has the same slopes in the $x$ and $y$ directions as the paraboloid does at that point.

Mean Value Theorem for Partial Derivatives

For a one-parameter function $g$, we have the classical mean value theorem: if $g$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and $g'$ is continuous, then there exists $c\in (a,b)$ such that:

$$g'(c) = \frac{g(x)-g(a)}{x-a} \Leftrightarrow g(x)-g(a) = g'(c)(x-a) = g'(a)(x-a) + o(|x-a|)$$

For two-parameter functions $f(x,y)$, we have the following extensions of the mean value theorem:

$$f(x,b)-f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)(x-a)+o(|x-a|)$$ $$f(a,y)-f(a,b) = f_y(a,b)(y-b)+o(|y-b|)$$

Proof of the Mean Value Theorem for Partial Derivatives

We'll prove both equations by applying the one-variable mean value theorem.

For the first equation, fix $y = b$ and define the function $g(x) = f(x,b)$. Then $g'(x) = f_x(x,b)$.

By the one-variable mean value theorem, there exists $c \in (a,x)$ (or $c \in (x,a)$ if $x < a$) such that:

$$g(x) - g(a) = g'(c)(x-a)$$

Substituting with our definitions:

$$f(x,b) - f(a,b) = f_x(c,b)(x-a)$$

Since $f_x$ is continuous at $(a,b)$, we have $f_x(c,b) = f_x(a,b) + o(1)$ as $c \to a$ (which happens as $x \to a$).

Therefore:

$$f(x,b) - f(a,b) = [f_x(a,b) + o(1)](x-a) = f_x(a,b)(x-a) + o(|x-a|)$$

The proof for the second equation is similar, fixing $x = a$ and working with $y$.

Justification of Tangent Planes

Theorem 3.1

Let $f(x,y)$ be a function defined on a neighborhood of $(a,b)$. If $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, then:

$$f(x,y)-f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b)+ o(|x-a|+|y-b|)$$

Proof of Theorem 3.1

We'll use the mean value theorem for partial derivatives that we just proved.

Step 1: First, we move from the point $(a,b)$ to the intermediate point $(x,b)$.

By the mean value theorem for partial derivatives:

$$f(x,b)-f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)(x-a)+o(|x-a|)$$

Step 2: Next, we move from $(x,b)$ to $(x,y)$.

Applying the mean value theorem with respect to $y$, with the point $(x,b)$ as our base:

$$f(x,y)-f(x,b) = f_y(x,b)(y-b)+o(|y-b|)$$

Since $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, the partial derivative $f_y$ is continuous near $(a,b)$, so:

$$f_y(x,b) = f_y(a,b) + o(1)$$ as $x \to a$

Therefore:

\begin{align*} f(x,y)-f(x,b) &= [f_y(a,b) + o(1)](y-b)+o(|y-b|)\\ &= f_y(a,b)(y-b) + o(1)(y-b) + o(|y-b|)\\ &= f_y(a,b)(y-b) + o(|y-b|) + o(|x-a|)|y-b| \end{align*}

The term $o(|x-a|)|y-b|$ can be absorbed into $o(|x-a|+|y-b|)$ since:

\begin{align*} o(|x-a|)|y-b| &\leq o(|x-a|)(|x-a|+|y-b|)\\ &= o((|x-a|+|y-b|)^2)\\ &= o(|x-a|+|y-b|) \end{align*}

Step 3: Finally, we combine these steps.

Adding the equations from Steps 1 and 2:

\begin{align*} f(x,y) - f(a,b) &= [f(x,b)-f(a,b)] + [f(x,y)-f(x,b)]\\ &= f_x(a,b)(x-a) + o(|x-a|) + f_y(a,b)(y-b) + o(|y-b|)\\ &= f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b) + o(|x-a|+|y-b|) \end{align*}

This completes the proof.

Key Insights about Tangent Planes

  • The tangent plane gives the best linear approximation to a surface at a point.
  • The equation of the tangent plane depends only on the point of tangency and the partial derivatives at that point.
  • Geometrically, the tangent plane touches the surface at exactly one point (locally).
  • The error in the tangent plane approximation goes to zero faster than the distance from the point.
  • Just as tangent lines are fundamental to single-variable calculus, tangent planes are essential for multivariable calculus, especially for approximation and optimization problems.
