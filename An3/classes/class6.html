Class 6: Differentiation in Polar Coordinates

In this class, we explore how to perform differentiation in polar coordinates, applying the chain rule to relate derivatives in polar coordinates to those in Cartesian coordinates. This is especially useful for radially symmetric functions and problems with circular symmetry.

Introduction to Polar Coordinates

Polar Coordinate System

Polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ relate to Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$ via the transformation:

\begin{align*} x &= r\cos\theta\\ y &= r\sin\theta \end{align*}

Conversely, we can express polar coordinates in terms of Cartesian coordinates:

\begin{align*} r &= \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\\ \theta &= \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \end{align*}

When working with functions in polar coordinates, we often encounter expressions of the form $f(r, \theta)$ where $r$ represents the distance from the origin and $\theta$ represents the angle from the positive $x$-axis.

Chain Rule for Polar Coordinates

To differentiate a function expressed in polar coordinates, we apply the chain rule from Class 5. If $z = f(r, \theta)$ and we want to find the partial derivatives with respect to $x$ and $y$, we use:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}$$
$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y}$$

We need to compute the derivatives of $r$ and $\theta$ with respect to $x$ and $y$:

Derivatives of Polar-to-Cartesian Conversion

From $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, we get:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} &= \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} = \frac{x}{r} = \cos\theta\\ \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} &= \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}} = \frac{y}{r} = \sin\theta \end{align*}

From $\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$, we get:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} &= \frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2} = -\frac{y}{r^2} = -\frac{\sin\theta}{r}\\ \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} &= \frac{x}{x^2 + y^2} = \frac{x}{r^2} = \frac{\cos\theta}{r} \end{align*}

Substituting these results into our chain rule formulas, we get:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial r} \cdot \cos\theta - \frac{\partial z}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{r}$$
$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial r} \cdot \sin\theta + \frac{\partial z}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{r}$$
Example 6.1

Consider the function $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$. Express this function in polar coordinates and find $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ using both direct Cartesian differentiation and the chain rule with polar coordinates.

Solution:

In polar coordinates, $f(r,\theta) = r^2$, since $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$.

Using direct Cartesian differentiation:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2 + y^2) = 2x\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(x^2 + y^2) = 2y \end{align*}

Using the chain rule with polar coordinates:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2) = 2r\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(r^2) = 0 \end{align*}

Now applying our formulas:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \cos\theta - \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r \cdot \cos\theta - 0 \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r\cos\theta \end{align*}

Since $r\cos\theta = x$, we have:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2r\cos\theta = 2x \end{align*}

Similarly:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \sin\theta + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r \cdot \sin\theta + 0 \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r\sin\theta = 2y \end{align*}

This confirms that both methods yield the same result.

Example 6.2

Find the partial derivatives of $f(r,\theta) = r^2\sin(2\theta)$ with respect to $x$ and $y$.

Solution:

First, we compute the partial derivatives with respect to $r$ and $\theta$:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial r}(r^2\sin(2\theta)) = 2r\sin(2\theta)\\ \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}(r^2\sin(2\theta)) = r^2 \cdot 2\cos(2\theta) = 2r^2\cos(2\theta) \end{align*}

Using the chain rule:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x}\\ &= 2r\sin(2\theta) \cdot \cos\theta - 2r^2\cos(2\theta) \cdot \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r\sin(2\theta)\cos\theta - 2r\cos(2\theta)\sin\theta \end{align*}

Using the double-angle formula $\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= 2r \cdot 2\sin\theta\cos\theta \cdot \cos\theta - 2r\cos(2\theta)\sin\theta\\ &= 4r\sin\theta\cos^2\theta - 2r\cos(2\theta)\sin\theta \end{align*}

Similarly for $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$:

\begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial r} \cdot \frac{\partial r}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y}\\ &= 2r\sin(2\theta) \cdot \sin\theta + 2r^2\cos(2\theta) \cdot \frac{\cos\theta}{r}\\ &= 2r\sin(2\theta)\sin\theta + 2r\cos(2\theta)\cos\theta \end{align*}

These expressions can be further simplified using trigonometric identities, but this demonstrates the process of differentiation in polar coordinates.

Applications of Polar Coordinates

Polar coordinates are particularly useful in problems involving:

  • Circular Symmetry - Problems where properties depend only on the distance from the origin (e.g., wave propagation from a point source)
  • Radial Fields - Gravitational, electric, or magnetic fields that vary with the distance from a central point
  • Rotation-Invariant Systems - Systems that behave the same regardless of orientation around the origin
  • Geometric Problems - Situations where radius and angle are more natural variables than Cartesian coordinates

Key Insights about Polar Coordinates

  • Polar coordinates often simplify problems with circular or radial symmetry that would be more complex in Cartesian coordinates.
  • The chain rule is essential for converting derivatives between coordinate systems.
  • Functions that have a simple form in one coordinate system may have a more complex representation in another.
  • When differentiating functions in polar form, remember that both $r$ and $\theta$ depend on $x$ and $y$.
  • Engineers commonly use polar coordinates for problems involving circular motion, wave propagation, and field analysis.
