Class 11: The Inverse Function Theorem

In previous classes, we studied how to differentiate vector-valued functions and introduced the Jacobian matrix. Today, we'll explore a powerful result called the Inverse Function Theorem, which provides conditions under which a vector-valued function has a differentiable inverse, and tells us how to compute derivatives of that inverse.

Let's begin by recalling the single-variable case. If $y = f(x)$ is a differentiable function and $f'(a) \neq 0$ at a point $a$, then near this point, $f$ has a differentiable inverse function $x = g(y)$ such that $g(f(x)) = x$. Moreover, the derivative of this inverse function is given by:

$$g'(b) = \frac{1}{f'(a)} \quad \text{where } b = f(a)$$

For example, if $f(x) = x^3 + x$, then $f'(x) = 3x^2 + 1 > 0$ for all $x$, so $f$ is strictly increasing and has an inverse function $g$. At any point, say $x=2$, we have $f'(2) = 3 \cdot 2^2 + 1 = 13$. Thus, if $b = f(2) = 2^3 + 2 = 10$, then $g'(10) = \frac{1}{13}$. This is the slope of the inverse function at the corresponding point.

The Inverse Function Theorem generalizes this result to vector-valued functions (mappings) between spaces of the same dimension. Suppose we have a mapping $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^n \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^n$ that is continuously differentiable in a neighborhood of a point $\mathbf{a}$. Let $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})$. The theorem states:

If the Jacobian determinant $\det(J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})) \neq 0$, then:

  1. There exists a neighborhood $U$ of $\mathbf{a}$ and a neighborhood $V$ of $\mathbf{b}$ such that $\mathbf{F}: U \rightarrow V$ is one-to-one and onto (i.e., invertible).
  2. The inverse function $\mathbf{G}: V \rightarrow U$ defined by $\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})) = \mathbf{x}$ for $\mathbf{x} \in U$ is continuously differentiable.
  3. The Jacobian matrix of $\mathbf{G}$ at $\mathbf{b}$ is the matrix inverse of the Jacobian matrix of $\mathbf{F}$ at $\mathbf{a}$:
$$J_\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{b}) = [J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})]^{-1}$$

In other words, a non-zero Jacobian determinant at a point guarantees that the mapping is locally invertible, and it provides a formula for the derivative of the inverse. This is a powerful result with numerous applications in calculus, differential equations, and mathematical modeling.

Example 11.1: Finding Derivatives of an Inverse Mapping

Question: Consider the transformation $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^2 \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^2$ given by $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (u,v)$ where $u = x^2 - y^2$ and $v = 2xy$. Determine if this mapping is locally invertible at the point $(3,2)$, and if so, find the partial derivatives of the inverse mapping at the corresponding point.

Solution:

First, we calculate the Jacobian matrix of $\mathbf{F}$ at the point $(3,2)$:

$$J_\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial v}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial v}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2x & -2y \\ 2y & 2x \end{pmatrix}$$

At the point $(3,2)$:

$$J_\mathbf{F}(3,2) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \cdot 3 & -2 \cdot 2 \\ 2 \cdot 2 & 2 \cdot 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & -4 \\ 4 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

Now we calculate the determinant of this Jacobian matrix:

$$\det(J_\mathbf{F}(3,2)) = 6 \cdot 6 - (-4) \cdot 4 = 36 + 16 = 52$$

Since the determinant is non-zero (specifically, $52 \neq 0$), the Inverse Function Theorem tells us that $\mathbf{F}$ is locally invertible at $(3,2)$. Let's find the image point:

$$\mathbf{F}(3,2) = (3^2 - 2^2, 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 2) = (9 - 4, 12) = (5, 12)$$

Now we can find the Jacobian matrix of the inverse function $\mathbf{G}$ at $(5,12)$ by inverting $J_\mathbf{F}(3,2)$:

$$J_\mathbf{G}(5,12) = [J_\mathbf{F}(3,2)]^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(J_\mathbf{F}(3,2))} \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 4 \\ -4 & 6 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{52} \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 4 \\ -4 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

This gives us the partial derivatives of the inverse mapping at $(5,12)$:

$$\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}(5,12) = \frac{6}{52} = \frac{3}{26}, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}(5,12) = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{2}{26} = \frac{1}{13}$$ $$\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}(5,12) = \frac{-4}{52} = \frac{-2}{26} = \frac{-1}{13}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial v}(5,12) = \frac{6}{52} = \frac{3}{26}$$

These derivatives describe how small changes in $(u,v)$ near $(5,12)$ affect the corresponding values of $(x,y)$ in the inverse mapping.

It's worth noting that we can actually find the explicit form of the inverse function for the example above. Setting $u = x^2 - y^2$ and $v = 2xy$, we recognize this as the transformation from Cartesian to "modified polar" coordinates, where $u$ and $v$ relate to $r^2$ and the sine/cosine of the angle. With some algebra, we can derive:

$$x = \pm\sqrt{\frac{u + \sqrt{u^2 + v^2}}{2}}, \quad y = \pm\frac{v}{2x}$$

But the beauty of the Inverse Function Theorem is that we don't need to know the explicit form of the inverse to compute its derivatives; we can do so directly from the original function's Jacobian matrix.

Example 11.2: Analyzing Local Invertibility

Question: Consider the mapping $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (e^x \cos y, e^x \sin y)$. Determine where this mapping is locally invertible.

Solution:

We compute the Jacobian matrix:

$$J_\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial (e^x \cos y)}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial (e^x \cos y)}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial (e^x \sin y)}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial (e^x \sin y)}{\partial y} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} e^x \cos y & -e^x \sin y \\ e^x \sin y & e^x \cos y \end{pmatrix}$$

The determinant of this matrix is:

$$\det(J_\mathbf{F}(x,y)) = (e^x \cos y)(e^x \cos y) - (-e^x \sin y)(e^x \sin y) = e^{2x}(\cos^2 y + \sin^2 y) = e^{2x}$$

Since $e^{2x} > 0$ for all values of $x$, the determinant is never zero. According to the Inverse Function Theorem, the mapping $\mathbf{F}$ is locally invertible at every point in its domain!

In fact, this mapping represents the transformation from Cartesian coordinates $(x,y)$ to polar-like coordinates, where $u = e^x \cos y$ and $v = e^x \sin y$. The exponential factor $e^x$ represents a radial scaling that ensures the mapping is one-to-one locally everywhere.

The Inverse Function Theorem has numerous applications in mathematics and engineering:

Applications of the Inverse Function Theorem

  • Coordinate Transformations: The theorem provides a rigorous foundation for changing between different coordinate systems.
  • Solving Systems of Equations: The theorem guarantees that certain nonlinear systems can be solved uniquely in a neighborhood.
  • Implicit Function Theorem: This important result, which we'll study next, is derived using the Inverse Function Theorem.
  • Diffeomorphisms in Differential Geometry: The theorem helps characterize when maps between manifolds preserve differentiable structure.
  • Control Theory: The theorem appears in the analysis of the controllability of nonlinear systems.

In summary, the Inverse Function Theorem tells us when a vector-valued function has a differentiable inverse and provides a simple formula for computing derivatives of that inverse. The key condition is a non-zero Jacobian determinant, which ensures the linear approximation of the function is invertible. This powerful result extends the familiar relationship between derivatives of inverse functions from single-variable calculus to the multivariable setting.

Example 11.3: A Practical Application

Question: In a chemical reaction, the concentration $c_1$ of reactant A and the concentration $c_2$ of reactant B are related to the temperature $T$ and pressure $P$ by the equations:

$$c_1 = \frac{T^2}{P} + 2P, \quad c_2 = T - \frac{P^2}{T}$$

If we know that at a certain point, changing the temperature affects the concentration $c_1$ at a rate of $\frac{\partial c_1}{\partial T} = 4$ and the concentration $c_2$ at a rate of $\frac{\partial c_2}{\partial T} = 2$, how do small changes in concentrations $c_1$ and $c_2$ affect the temperature and pressure?

Solution:

Let $\mathbf{F}(T, P) = (c_1, c_2)$. We're given the partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial c_1}{\partial T} = 4, \quad \frac{\partial c_2}{\partial T} = 2$$

We also need to find $\frac{\partial c_1}{\partial P}$ and $\frac{\partial c_2}{\partial P}$. Given the equations, we can compute:

$$\frac{\partial c_1}{\partial P} = \frac{\partial}{\partial P}\left(\frac{T^2}{P} + 2P\right) = -\frac{T^2}{P^2} + 2$$ $$\frac{\partial c_2}{\partial P} = \frac{\partial}{\partial P}\left(T - \frac{P^2}{T}\right) = -\frac{2P}{T}$$

Now we have the Jacobian matrix:

$$J_\mathbf{F}(T,P) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial c_1}{\partial T} & \frac{\partial c_1}{\partial P} \\ \frac{\partial c_2}{\partial T} & \frac{\partial c_2}{\partial P} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -\frac{T^2}{P^2} + 2 \\ 2 & -\frac{2P}{T} \end{pmatrix}$$

By the Inverse Function Theorem, the Jacobian matrix of the inverse function (how T and P change with respect to $c_1$ and $c_2$) is the inverse of this matrix. To find it:

$$\det(J_\mathbf{F}) = 4 \cdot \left(-\frac{2P}{T}\right) - 2 \cdot \left(-\frac{T^2}{P^2} + 2\right) = -\frac{8P}{T} + 2\frac{T^2}{P^2} - 4$$

Assuming this determinant is non-zero, the inverse Jacobian is:

$$J_\mathbf{G}(c_1, c_2) = \frac{1}{\det(J_\mathbf{F})} \begin{pmatrix} -\frac{2P}{T} & \frac{T^2}{P^2} - 2 \\ -2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix gives us the rates of change of $T$ and $P$ with respect to $c_1$ and $c_2$. Without knowing the specific values of $T$ and $P$, we cannot simplify further, but this illustrates how the Inverse Function Theorem applies to practical scenarios involving multiple interrelated variables.

The Inverse Function Theorem demonstrates a deep connection between invertibility and differentiation in multivariable settings. The condition that the Jacobian determinant must be non-zero can be interpreted geometrically: it ensures that the mapping doesn't collapse dimensions locally (which would make the inverse impossible). This insight forms the foundation for many advanced concepts in calculus and differential geometry.
