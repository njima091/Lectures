Class 2: Planes and Surfaces

In this class, we study how planes can be seen as graphs of linear functions and how more general surfaces can be understood via level sets and cross-sections. These geometric interpretations help visualize abstract mathematical concepts.

Lines and Curves in 2D

Lines in 2D

Given a point $P=(x_0,y_0)$ and a normal vector $\mathbf{n} = (a,b)$, we consider a line orthogonal to $(a,b)$ passing through $P$ defined by the set of points $Q=(x,y)$ satisfying:

$$(Q-P)\cdot (a,b) = (x-x_0,y-y_0)\cdot(a,b) = 0$$

The line is equivalent to the set of points $(x,y)$ satisfying $y = -\frac{a}{b}(x-x_0) + y_0$ (assuming $b \neq 0$).

A curve in the $XY$-plane is typically defined by an equation $y=f(x)$ or implicitly as $F(x,y)=0$.

Examples of curves:

  • • Parabola: $y=x^2$
  • • Circle: $y = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2}$ or $x^2 + y^2 = 1$

Planes in 3D Space

In three-dimensional space, the graph of a function $z=f(x,y)$ is a surface. The simplest examples of surfaces are planes given by linear equations.

General Equation of a Plane

A general plane has equation:

$$Ax + By + Cz + D = 0$$

where $(A,B,C)$ is a normal vector perpendicular to the plane.

If $C \neq 0$, we can solve for $z$ to write the plane in the form:

$$z = mx + ny + p$$

Here, $m = -A/C$ represents the slope in the $x$-direction, $n = -B/C$ represents the slope in the $y$-direction, and $p = -D/C$ is the $z$-intercept.

Example 2.1

Consider the plane $z = 2x - 3y + 5$

This is a plane with slope $2$ in the $x$-direction and slope $-3$ in the $y$-direction. The $z$-intercept is $5$.

The corresponding general form is $-2x + 3y + z - 5 = 0$, with normal vector $(-2, 3, 1)$.

Surfaces in 3D Space

A surface in three-dimensional space is the graph of a function $z=f(x,y)$. In other words, the surface is the set of points $(x,y,z)$ satisfying $z=f(x,y)$.

Common Surfaces

  1. Paraboloid: $z = x^2 + y^2$ [View Animation]
  2. Cone: $z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ [View Animation]
  3. Saddle surface: $z = y^2 - x^2$ or $y = -x^2 + z^2$ [View Animation]

Contour Lines (Level Curves) for Surfaces

Let $f(x, y)$ be a real-valued function of two variables. The graph of $f$ is the surface in 3D space given by $z = f(x, y)$. To study this surface using a 2D representation, we often use contour lines (also called level curves).

Contour Line (Level Curve)

A contour line (or level curve) of $f(x, y)$ corresponding to the value $c$ is the set of all points $(x, y)$ such that:

$$f(x, y) = c$$

This is a curve in the $xy$-plane. The full contour map is the collection of these curves for several values of $c$.

For example, the contour lines for $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ with $c = 0, 1, 2$ are circles centered at the origin with radii $0, 1, \sqrt{2}$, respectively.

Example 2.2

Question: Find the contour lines at level $f(x, y) = 0$ for the following functions:

a) $f(x, y) = \sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2}$

Setting $f(x, y) = 0$:

$$\sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2} = 0$$ $$1 - x^2 - y^2 = 0$$ $$x^2 + y^2 = 1$$

This is a circle of radius 1 centered at the origin.

b) $f(x, y) = 1 - x + 2y$

Setting $f(x, y) = 0$:

$$1 - x + 2y = 0$$ $$x = 1 + 2y$$

This is a straight line with slope 2 and $x$-intercept at 1.

Key Insights about Contour Lines

  • Contour lines connect points of equal height (or value) on a surface.
  • Closely spaced contour lines indicate steep regions of the surface.
  • Widely spaced contour lines indicate flat regions.
  • Contour lines never cross (except at saddle points when projected onto the xy-plane).
  • Contour maps are widely used in topography, weather maps, and engineering analysis.
